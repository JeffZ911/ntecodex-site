Players frequently ask how the Elemental Resonance system, officially termed the Esper Cycle, actually functions during high-stakes encounters. The system is built around three core questions:

*   **What is the Elemental Resonance system (Esper Cycle) in Neverness to Everness?** It is a mechanical framework where character elements (Esper Types) interact based on their position on a hexagonal wheel to trigger team-wide buffs and combat effects.
*   **How do different elements interact to trigger team-wide buffs?** Interactions occur when you swap from an active character to a standby character whose element is "adjacent" on the Esper Wheel, provided the Esper Meter is fully charged.
*   **What are the specific requirements to activate Duo and Trio reactions?** Duo reactions require a two-element sequence between neighbors (e.g., Cosmos to Anima). Trio reactions, such as Discord or Charged, require a three-element sequence involving specific "lanes" on the wheel.

### Defining the Esper Cycle System

The Esper Cycle is the engine of NTE’s combat. Every character is assigned one of six Esper Types. As you deal damage with Normal Attacks, use Resonance Skills, or perform a "Perfect Parry," you accumulate energy in the Esper Meter. Once this meter is full, the character's portrait glows, signaling they are ready to "Cycle" in. Swapping at this precise moment triggers the resonance effect associated with the elemental pairing.

### The Importance of Elemental Adjacency

The most critical rule in NTE is the Adjacency Rule. The six elements are arranged in a fixed hexagon. A reaction only occurs if you swap between two elements that share a border on this wheel. For example, if you swap from a Lakshana character to a Cosmos character, you trigger a reaction. However, swapping from Lakshana to Chaos (which are opposite each other) results in a "Broken Cycle," providing no resonance bonus and significantly lowering your total team DPS.

### Optimizing Team Compositions: The 'Elemental Lane' Strategy

The most effective teams follow an "Elemental Lane"—a string of 3 adjacent elements.
